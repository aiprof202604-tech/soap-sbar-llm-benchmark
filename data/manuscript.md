# Fact-Transcription Fidelity in Large Language Model SOAP-to-SBAR Translation: A Three-Stage Automated Evaluation Across Three Vendors

## Abstract

**Background.** Large language models (LLMs) are increasingly used in free-text clinical documentation, including translation of SOAP (Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan) records into SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) handoff summaries; evaluation methodology has lagged behind deployment. Single-judge LLM-as-a-Judge scoring inherits the biases of the chosen judge, while expert clinical adjudication does not scale.

**Objective.** To develop a three-stage automated evaluation pipeline for free-text clinical-document generation, and to apply it to the first systematic comparison of fact-transcription fidelity across three commercial LLMs in SOAP-to-SBAR translation.

**Methods.** Nine fictional SOAP scenarios authored *de novo* and stratified across acute, chronic, and palliative-care categories (three per category) elicited 1,620 SBAR records from GPT-4o, Claude Opus 4.5, and Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite (3 models × 9 scenarios × 2 sampling temperatures × 30 trials). Each SBAR-by-tag pair, drawn from 180 pre-specified fact tags stratified by preservation difficulty, was evaluated by deterministic rule-based matching, by two independent AI judges from different vendors, and by arbiter resolution from a third LLM. Inter-judge reliability, within-condition reproducibility, and an alternative-arbiter sensitivity analysis were reported as transparency diagnostics.

**Results.** All 1,620 records (100%) entered analysis. Mean preservation, pooled across temperatures, was 0.568 for Gemini, 0.510 for Claude, and 0.374 for GPT-4o; the rank ordering held at each temperature and under sensitivity analysis. The cross-vendor effect on preservation rate was statistically substantial (Friedman χ²(2) = 11.556, p = 0.0031, Kendall's W = 0.642; Bonferroni-corrected pairwise Wilcoxon, Gemini > GPT-4o p = 0.012, Claude > GPT-4o p = 0.023, Gemini vs Claude p = 0.293). Within-model temperature effects were modest (median shifts ≤ 0.031) and did not survive Bonferroni correction. Stage 2 inter-judge agreement was moderate to substantial (unweighted Cohen's κ = 0.516; linear-weighted κ = 0.619; n = 16,560 paired cells); within-condition reproducibility was high (ICC(2,1) ≥ 0.852). Detected hallucinations were elevated for Claude and concentrated in the Background section across all three vendors.

**Conclusions.** The pipeline supports tractable and reproducible evaluation of fact-transcription fidelity without recurrent expert adjudication. Vendor selection materially influences fidelity, which is proposed here as a complementary evaluation axis to accuracy in the responsible appraisal of LLMs for clinical documentation.

**Keywords:** large language models; clinical documentation; SBAR; nursing handoff; fact-transcription fidelity; hallucination; LLM-as-a-Judge; natural language processing; reproducibility of results; medical informatics.

## 1. Introduction

Structured documentation and structured handoff are foundational to modern clinical practice. The SOAP (Subjective, Objective, Assessment, Plan) record, introduced by Weed [1], is the canonical format for narrative documentation in nursing and medical education. The SBAR (Situation, Background, Assessment, Recommendation) framework, disseminated by Haig and colleagues [2], is widely taught as the standard format for concise inter-clinician communication, particularly during nursing handoffs and rapid-response activations; a recent systematic review of structured documentation frameworks reaffirms its role in reducing communication-related incidents and supporting patient safety [3]. The two formats serve complementary functions: SOAP captures comprehensive narrative detail at a single moment of care, whereas SBAR distils that detail into a prioritised, action-oriented summary fit for transfer under time pressure. Translating SOAP into SBAR is therefore a cognitively demanding clinical task that nurses perform repeatedly across a working day.

Generative artificial intelligence has begun to enter nursing and medical education at considerable pace. Med-PaLM has been reported to perform encouragingly on professional medical question-answering benchmarks while remaining inferior to clinicians [4]; ChatGPT-4 has been reported to achieve 88.7% accuracy on NCLEX-RN practical questions [5]. Beyond accuracy on multiple-choice items, LLMs are now deployed in nursing-education contexts as virtual patients, formative-assessment tutors, and reflective-feedback agents [6]. A consequence of this rapid uptake is that evaluation methodology has had to evolve: a single accuracy figure on a closed-form item set is insufficient to characterise an educational tool whose principal output is free text. The closest precedent for LLM-generated clinical handoff evaluation is Hartman and colleagues [7], who evaluated LLM-generated emergency-medicine-to-inpatient handoff notes against physician-written notes; that evaluation, however, relied on physician scoring and the source-target pairing differed from SOAP-to-SBAR translation.

Evaluating free-text LLM output is substantively more complex than evaluating multiple-choice answers. Whereas a single-letter response permits deterministic matching, a free-text SBAR does not. The dominant solution is the LLM-as-a-Judge paradigm, in which one LLM scores another's output against a rubric [8]. Single-judge LLM scoring inherits two well-documented problems: the judge brings its own biases, including a tendency to favour outputs resembling its own generation patterns; and on genuinely ambiguous items, a binary verdict is brittle and unreproducible. Engaging an expert clinical adjudicator on every item is constrained by limited scalability and inter-rater reliability, and may be unnecessary if the construct of interest is fact-transcription rather than clinical adequacy. No published study, to the author's knowledge, has systematically characterised LLM SOAP-to-SBAR translation fidelity, nor paired free-text clinical-document evaluation with a multi-stage automated judging architecture combining deterministic matching, multi-judge AI scoring, and arbiter resolution.

This work addresses these gaps with three contributions. The principal methodological contribution is a three-stage automated evaluation pipeline for free-text clinical-document generation: a deterministic rule-based first stage covering machine-verifiable facts, two independent AI judges in the second stage with reported pairwise Cohen's κ, and an arbiter LLM in the third stage resolving disagreements through a deterministic majority rule. The pipeline is task-, model-, and language-agnostic. The second contribution is the first systematic SOAP-to-SBAR fidelity benchmark across three commercial LLMs (GPT-4o, Claude Opus 4.5, Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite) under a fully crossed factorial design over two sampling temperatures and nine clinical scenarios spanning acute, chronic, and palliative-care categories. The third contribution is a set of empirical observations on preservation rate, hallucination distribution by SBAR section, and cross-temperature stability, contributing standardised reproducibility metrics. Fact-transcription fidelity is the scope of this work; whether the SBAR is clinically appropriate, in addition to being faithful, is explicitly outside scope and deferred to future work.

## 2. Materials and Methods

### 2.1 Study design

This is a fully crossed factorial computational experiment. The independent variables were model (3 levels), sampling temperature (2 levels), and clinical scenario (9 levels, three per category); the dependent variables were SOAP-to-SBAR generation outputs and their downstream preservation and hallucination scores. With 30 trials per cell, the total number of planned API calls was 3 × 2 × 9 × 30 = 1,620. Cell-level n = 30 was set to characterise within-cell stochastic variability (yielding ICC(2,1) ≥ 0.852 in §3.2); nine scenarios afford exploratory category-level analysis while keeping the total API budget tractable, with the explicit acknowledgement (§4.4, limitation 3) that this n affords low statistical power for category-level inference. The study evaluates LLM behaviour and involves no human or animal subjects, no identifiable patient data, and no clinical specimens; institutional ethics review was therefore not required. The transparency principles of TRIPOD-LLM [9] informed §§2.3–2.5; CONSORT-AI [10], MI-CLAIM [11], and MI-CLAIM-GEN [12] were consulted for relevant items, recognising that those instruments target prospective clinical evaluations rather than the present computational benchmark.

### 2.2 Source SOAP scenarios and fact tags

Nine fictional SOAP records were authored *de novo* by the principal investigator, a nursing faculty member with clinical-education expertise. Three records were authored per clinical category: **acute care** (A1 acute pain, post-laparoscopic appendectomy on postoperative day 1; A2 deficient fluid volume, geriatric heat-related dehydration; A3 impaired gas exchange, chronic obstructive pulmonary disease acute exacerbation); **chronic care** (C1 readiness for enhanced self-management, diabetes educational admission; C2 activity intolerance, heart-failure pre-discharge; C3 chronic pain, pre-total-knee-arthroplasty); and **palliative care** (P1 anxiety, pancreatic-cancer chemotherapy; P2 caregiver role strain, lung-cancer home transition; P3 spiritual distress, ovarian-cancer palliative care unit). Each record comprised approximately 700 to 1,200 Japanese characters of body content (mean 947) distributed across the four SOAP sections, with palliative-care scenarios slightly longer on average because of the greater density of psychosocial detail. NANDA-International nursing-diagnosis labels [13] served as a conceptual reference framework but did not constrain the categorical design.

Twenty pre-specified clinical fact tags were identified per scenario, yielding 180 tags in total. Each tag carried four annotations: (a) content domain (vitals, laboratory, symptoms, treatment, psychosocial, or plan); (b) clinical priority (high, medium, low); (c) the SBAR section in which it would most naturally appear; and (d) preservation difficulty — *easy* (88 tags: explicit numeric values, named drugs, named laboratory tests, named scales), *medium* (74 tags: paraphrasable facts requiring simple semantic matching), or *hard* (18 tags: interpretive descriptions of psychosocial circumstances, motivations, or values). The preservation-difficulty stratum determined which judging stage applied. The full scenario set, tag set, and annotations are openly released as supplementary files.

### 2.3 LLM generation

Three commercial-API vendors widely deployed in current clinical-AI evaluation work — OpenAI, Anthropic, and Google — were represented by GPT-4o (snapshot `gpt-4o-2024-08-06`), Claude Opus 4.5 (snapshot `claude-opus-4-5-20251101`), and Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite (queried via the moving alias `gemini-2.5-flash-lite`). Snapshots were selected for production-tier stability and external-benchmarking continuity; Anthropic's later Opus 4.7 (released 15 April 2026) was not adopted because it imposes server-side restrictions on non-default sampling parameters that would have precluded the multi-temperature factorial design. For OpenAI and Anthropic, the API string identifies a fixed snapshot, and the `resolved_model` field confirmed exact snapshot resolution for all 540 records per generation arm. For Google, the API does not expose a build-specific snapshot identifier: the `resolved_model` field echoed the queried alias for all 540 Gemini records, recorded as a known transparency limitation of the Google AI API. Reproducers querying the OpenAI and Anthropic snapshots will receive responses from the same underlying models; the Gemini alias may resolve to a different build depending on the production-build pointer at that date. Per-call `resolved_model` values and full response logs are released with the repository. Commercial production APIs were preferred over open-weight models because they provide a standardised inference environment without additional variance from fine-tuning, quantisation, or inference-stack configuration; extension to open-weight models is identified as future work (§4.5).

Each model was queried at two sampling temperatures (T = 0.0 and T = 1.0; "temperature" hereafter denotes the LLM API stochasticity parameter, distinct from clinical body temperature, itself one of the observations to be preserved during translation). T = 0.0 is the lowest setting each API exposes but does not guarantee bit-exact determinism in commercial endpoints; observed within-cell variability at T = 0.0, characterised by ICC(2,1) in §3.2, reflects this residual non-determinism alongside infrastructure-level variation. Endpoint-only designs are standard in factorial experimentation when the question of interest is the presence or absence of a monotonic effect [14]; non-monotonic patterns cannot be characterised, as acknowledged in §4.4. All other sampling parameters were held fixed: `top_p` = 1.0, `max_tokens` = 400, and `presence_penalty` and `frequency_penalty` at default. No random seed was set. A single Japanese prompt — instructing the model to act as a ward nurse, to produce SBAR with each section limited to 50 words, and to add no information absent from the source SOAP — was used for every call; the full prompt is provided as a supplementary file. Calls were issued in parallel from a single Python 3.12 client environment (`openai` and `anthropic` SDKs; `requests` to the Gemini REST endpoint), with per-vendor parallelism tuned to documented rate limits; the exact configuration is recorded in the experiment-runner script released with the repository.

### 2.4 Three-stage automated judging pipeline

A three-stage automated pipeline was implemented to remove the principal investigator from the judging loop and to mitigate the single-judge bias inherent in standard LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation.

**Stage 1: rule-based check.** For each SBAR-by-tag pair in the *easy* stratum, a deterministic check evaluated whether the tag's factual content was preserved in the SBAR. Matching rules were authored in advance and applied identically to every cell; their full code is openly released. Two pattern types were used. For tags containing numeric tokens, all extracted numbers were required to appear in the SBAR; two special cases were implemented — scale-fraction tokens X/Y (such as "4/10" pain scores) were treated as X-only, since clinicians routinely drop the denominator, while blood-pressure tokens X/Y mmHg required preservation of both values. For tags containing recognised named entities (drug, test, and device names), the entity was required to appear in the SBAR. Stage 1 produced binary outcomes (0 = lost, 2 = preserved). Tags whose pattern could not be matched exactly were marked *undecidable* and escalated to Stage 2, so that false negatives in Stage 1 were absorbed without loss of information. The strict policy will, in unusual cases, return *not-preserved* where a numerically equivalent paraphrase has been substituted (e.g., "37.2°C" rendered as "low-grade fever in the 37°C range"); this conservatism is by design, since relaxing it would shift the construct from transcription fidelity to clinical equivalence (§4.4, limitation 1).

**Stage 2: independent AI judges.** For each SBAR-by-tag pair in the *medium* and *hard* strata — and any *easy* tags that Stage 1 could not decide — two LLMs scored independently, both at temperature 0.0: GPT-4o (snapshot `gpt-4o-2024-08-06`, identical to the generation-arm snapshot) and Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite (alias identical to the generation-arm alias). The intentional use of identical snapshots and aliases between generation and Stage 2 judging ensures that any vendor-specific behaviour observed in judging reflects the same underlying model state as generation, eliminating one source of confounding. Each judge received the source SOAP, the generated SBAR, the list of tags, and a three-level rubric (0 = lost, 1 = partial, 2 = fully preserved); each returned per-tag scores and a binary list of detected hallucinations, defined as SBAR sentences introducing discrete propositional content (a numeric value, a named entity, or an assertable clinical fact) not derivable from the source SOAP. The three-level rubric balanced resolution and inter-judge reliability: a binary rubric would have discarded the partial-preservation category (the modal outcome for paraphrastic medium-stratum tags during pilot inspection), whereas a five-level Likert rubric would in principle have provided finer resolution but is associated with lower inter-judge agreement on middle categories. Hallucinations were not graded for clinical severity, a deliberate scope restriction (§4.4, limitation 1). Inter-judge agreement on the three-level preservation rubric was reported as Cohen's κ.

Two of the three commercial vendors used in generation are also represented in Stage 2 judging — GPT-4o and Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite — raising the legitimate concern of self-evaluation bias, in which a judge model may favour outputs from its own vendor. This work does not eliminate the concern architecturally but exposes it to scrutiny in two ways. First, using two judges from different vendors means any vendor-specific bias affects only one judge per cell, and is therefore detectable in the pairwise inter-judge agreement (§3.2). Second, the arbiter stage and the alternative-arbiter sensitivity analysis (§3.5) both rely on Anthropic models not represented in Stage 2, providing an additional consensus-level check. Residual self-evaluation bias is acknowledged in §4.4 (limitation 3).

**Stage 3: arbiter for disagreements, with sensitivity analysis using an alternative arbiter.** For SBAR-by-tag cells in which the two Stage 2 judges returned different preservation scores, a third independent LLM was queried at temperature 0.0 for its own three-level score. The final consensus was determined by a unique-majority rule: where a single value held a strict plurality among the three judges, that value was adopted; otherwise the tie was broken by an upper-median rule (the value at the position equal to floor(n/2) in the sorted score list). This single rule covers both the three-way tie (resolved to 1) and the two-way tie when one judge score is unavailable (resolved to the higher of the two remaining values), and was implemented identically irrespective of the count of available scores. Concordant Stage 2 cells were passed through unchanged. The arbiter intervention rate was reported as a transparency diagnostic. A two-tier arbiter design was adopted: the primary arbiter (Claude Haiku 4.5, snapshot `claude-haiku-4-5-20251001`), applied to the full disagreement set, was selected for cost-efficiency; a secondary arbiter (Claude Opus 4.5, snapshot `claude-opus-4-5-20251101`, identical to the Claude generation-arm snapshot) was applied to a stratified random sample of 500 disagreement cells as a sensitivity check. Both arbiters share the Anthropic Claude family but differ substantially in capability tier — Haiku 4.5 the fastest cost-efficient tier, Opus 4.5 the most capable tier at the time of data collection (May 2026) [15]. The sensitivity analysis therefore tests whether model-level conclusions are robust to a substantial change in arbiter capability while holding the vendor-family bias constant. Cell-level agreement between the two arbiters and aggregate-level robustness of the model rankings under arbiter substitution are reported in §3.5.

### 2.5 Statistical analysis

For each (model × temperature) cell, the preservation rate was computed as the per-tag final score divided by 2 (mapping the underlying 0/1/2 scale onto the unit interval) and averaged across all tags. Stage 1 deterministic outcomes were combined with Stage 3 final consensus scores to give the unified preservation rate per cell. Cell-level descriptive statistics (mean, standard deviation, median, n) were tabulated by (model × temperature). Confidence intervals shown graphically (Figures 1 and 2) were computed by non-parametric bootstrap resampling at the case level (n = 9 cases per cell for Figure 1; n = 3 cases per category in Figure 2) with B = 10,000 replications and reported as the 2.5th and 97.5th percentiles; the case-level resampling unit is consistent with the unit of analysis used in the Wilcoxon and Kruskal–Wallis tests below.

The within-model effect of sampling temperature on preservation rate was tested using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test [16] on case-level paired means (n = 9 paired cases per model). Tied pairs were handled using SciPy's default policy, which omits zero differences from the rank computation; the number of non-zero pairs accompanies each W statistic in the Supplementary Material. The effect of clinical category was tested using the Kruskal–Wallis test on case-level mean preservation rate (one observation per case). The cross-vendor effect was tested formally using the Friedman test on the case-by-model matrix of preservation rates pooled across temperatures (n = 9 cases × 3 models), the appropriate non-parametric repeated-measures omnibus test in this setting; pairwise post-hoc comparisons between every model pair were conducted using the Wilcoxon signed-rank test on case-level paired means, with Bonferroni correction applied across the three pairs. Kendall's W is reported as the omnibus effect size for Friedman, and rank-biserial correlation as the effect size for each pairwise comparison. Inter-judge reliability for Stage 2 was quantified by Cohen's κ [17] across paired SBAR-by-tag cells in the medium and hard strata. Cross-temperature stability per model was characterised by ICC(2,1) [18] on the case-by-temperature matrix, following the original Shrout and Fleiss specification; treating each temperature level as a "rater" departs from the strict random-rater assumption, so ICC is reported here as a within-condition reproducibility diagnostic alongside the primary Wilcoxon test, not as a substitute for it. Hallucination detection per SBAR was computed as the *union* of sentences flagged by either Stage 2 judge, with sentences flagged by both counted only once. Per-SBAR tallies were aggregated to (model × SBAR-section) cells for Table 3 and to (model × case) cells for Figure 4. Two-sided tests were used throughout, with α = 0.05. Bonferroni correction was applied across two pre-specified families: (i) three within-model tests of the temperature effect, and (ii) three pairwise cross-vendor comparisons of preservation rate. Other secondary analyses (Kruskal–Wallis on category, ICC, sensitivity analysis) are descriptive and not incorporated into the family-wise correction. Effect sizes (rank-biserial r for Wilcoxon, Kendall's W for Friedman, η² for Kruskal–Wallis, raw mean differences for hallucination rates) were reported alongside p-values. All analyses were conducted in Python 3.12 using the standard scientific stack (NumPy, pandas, SciPy, scikit-learn); exact dependency versions are pinned in the `requirements.txt` file released with the repository.

## 3. Results

### 3.1 Sample characteristics and pipeline flow

A total of 1,620 SBAR records were generated through the commercial APIs. Data collection took place during the first week of May 2026 and concluded on 8 May 2026 at 06:22 (Asia/Tokyo). Collection proceeded in two phases: an initial parallel run, followed by a resumption phase in which cells that had failed in the initial run owing to API rate-limit responses were retried. The resumable design of the experiment runner ensured that only previously failed cells were re-queried, eliminating duplication. Both phases used identical model snapshots, prompts, and sampling parameters; the only changes between phases were operational (vendor tier upgrades to raise rate limits, and one Gemini API-key re-issue against a billing-account-linked project to remove free-tier quota constraints). After a post-generation parsing check, all 1,620 records (100%) were classified as valid (parseable into the four SBAR sections) and entered the evaluation pipeline.

The 180 pre-specified fact tags partitioned into 88 *easy* tags (49%), 74 *medium* tags (41%), and 18 *hard* tags (10%). Because each tag is case-specific and is evaluated against the 180 SBARs generated for that case (30 trials × 2 temperatures × 3 models), the resulting set of evaluable SBAR-by-tag cells comprises 88 × 180 = 15,840 in the *easy* stratum and 92 × 180 = 16,560 in the combined *medium* and *hard* strata. The *easy* stratum was evaluated deterministically in Stage 1 and produced a decisive verdict for 15,300 of the 15,840 cells (96.6%); the remaining 540 *undecidable* cells were escalated to Stage 2. The *medium* and *hard* strata were evaluated in their entirety by the two Stage 2 judges, generating 16,560 paired-judge cells subject to inter-judge agreement analysis (§3.2) and Stage 3 arbiter resolution where required. The unified final-consensus output therefore covers 31,860 SBAR-by-tag cells (15,300 from Stage 1 plus 16,560 from Stage 2/3), giving 5,310 evaluations per (model × temperature) combination reported in Table 1 (31,860 ÷ 6 = 5,310). Table 1 summarises preservation outcomes by model and temperature.

### 3.2 Reliability of the evaluation pipeline

Inter-judge reliability was substantively above chance but below the level that would justify reliance on any single judge. For the 16,560 SBAR-by-tag cells in the *medium* and *hard* strata, the two judges (GPT-4o and Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite) agreed in 11,344 cells (raw agreement 0.685); pairwise unweighted Cohen's κ was 0.516, conventionally interpreted as moderate agreement under Landis and Koch [19]. Because the rubric is ordinal and one-step disagreements (lost vs partial, partial vs fully preserved) are conceptually less severe than two-step disagreements (lost vs fully preserved), the linear-weighted Cohen's κ is also reported as the more appropriate single index for an ordinal three-level rubric: linear-weighted κ = 0.619, in the substantial-agreement range under the same benchmarks. Of the 5,216 cells on which the two judges disagreed, 4,474 (85.8%) involved a one-step (adjacent) disagreement and only 742 (14.2%) involved a two-step (extreme) disagreement; this distribution corroborates the linear-weighted κ as the more faithful summary of inter-judge concordance and indicates that the gap between the unweighted and linear-weighted indices arises from the well-known sensitivity of unweighted κ to ordinal one-step disagreements rather than from substantive contradiction between the judges. The arbiter (Claude Haiku 4.5) was therefore engaged on the 5,216 disagreement cells, an intervention rate of 31.5%. A single cell elicited a parse-failure response from the primary arbiter; for this cell the arbiter score was treated as missing and the final consensus was determined from the two Stage 2 scores using the upper-median tie-break rule of §2.4. The aggregate impact of this single fallback on every statistic reported in this paper is bounded by 1 in 16,560, less than 0.01% — negligible at every level of analysis.

Within-condition reproducibility, computed as ICC(2,1) on the case-by-temperature matrix (§2.5), was high for all three models: Claude Opus 4.5 = 0.974, Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite = 0.882, GPT-4o = 0.852. Reliability statistics are summarised in Table 2.

### 3.3 Fact preservation across models and temperatures

The three models differed substantially in fact-preservation rate (Table 1; Figure 1). Pooled across temperatures, mean preservation was highest for Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite (0.568), intermediate for Claude Opus 4.5 (0.510), and lowest for GPT-4o (0.374). The same ordering held at each temperature: at T = 0.0, Gemini reached 0.575 (SD = 0.470), Claude 0.517 (SD = 0.474), and GPT-4o 0.390 (SD = 0.458); at T = 1.0, the corresponding rates were 0.561 (SD = 0.471), 0.502 (SD = 0.471), and 0.358 (SD = 0.439). The model-level gap between Gemini and GPT-4o was approximately 0.20 in absolute preservation rate at both temperatures.

The cross-vendor effect on preservation rate was statistically substantial. The Friedman omnibus test on the case-by-model matrix of preservation rates pooled across temperatures (n = 9 cases × 3 models) yielded χ²(2) = 11.556, p = 0.0031, with Kendall's W = 0.642 indicating strong concordance of the case-level rankings. Bonferroni-corrected pairwise Wilcoxon signed-rank tests on case-level paired means localised the omnibus effect: Gemini exceeded GPT-4o in nine of nine cases (W = 0.0; Bonferroni-corrected p = 0.0117; rank-biserial r = +1.00), and Claude exceeded GPT-4o in eight of nine cases (W = 1.0; Bonferroni-corrected p = 0.0234; r = +0.96). The contrast between Gemini and Claude did not reach the conventional significance threshold (W = 8.0; Bonferroni-corrected p = 0.293; r = +0.64; sign-test p = 0.508). Cohen's d on case-level paired differences was +2.66 for Gemini-versus-GPT-4o, +1.47 for Claude-versus-GPT-4o, and +0.65 for Gemini-versus-Claude, indicating that the GPT-4o-versus-others contrast represents a very large effect on a continuous scale, while the Gemini-versus-Claude contrast represents an intermediate effect that the present nine-case design is underpowered to localise statistically.

The within-model effect of sampling temperature was small in magnitude. Wilcoxon signed-rank tests on case-level mean preservation (n = 9 paired cases per model) showed that no model exhibited a temperature effect surviving Bonferroni correction for the three pre-specified within-model tests. Uncorrected p-values were 0.047 (Claude), 0.547 (Gemini), and 0.098 (GPT-4o); after Bonferroni correction (uncorrected p × 3, with values exceeding unity capped at 1.000), the corrected values were 0.141, 1.000, and 0.294 respectively, all exceeding α = 0.05. Effect sizes (rank-biserial r) were +0.86 (Claude), +0.28 (Gemini), and +0.64 (GPT-4o), indicating that the *direction* of the temperature effect was internally consistent within each model even where the *magnitude* of the median shift was small. Median preservation differed between temperatures by 0.031 or less in every model: from 0.486 to 0.495 for Claude, from 0.553 to 0.584 for Gemini, and from 0.365 to 0.352 for GPT-4o. The direction of change was not uniform across vendors, rising slightly with temperature for Claude and Gemini and falling for GPT-4o. For Claude and Gemini the cell-level mean (Table 1) moved in the opposite direction to the case-level median, a divergence consistent with a small number of outlier scenarios in which T = 1.0 produced markedly lower preservation, to which the median is insensitive. A formal test of a model-by-temperature interaction was outside the scope of the present non-parametric analysis and is identified as a topic for future work (§4.5).

### 3.4 Hallucination detection

Hallucination detection rates differed sharply across the three models (Figure 3; Table 3). Following the protocol described in §2.5 (the union of sentences flagged by either Stage 2 judge per SBAR, with sentences flagged by both judges counted only once), Claude Opus 4.5 produced detected hallucinations at between 1.85 and 2.72 times the rate of the lowest-detecting model in each section (1.85× in Background, 2.03× in Situation, 2.45× in Recommendation, 2.72× in Assessment): 77 sentences per 100 SBARs in Situation, 126 in Background, 98 in Assessment, and 93 in Recommendation. GPT-4o produced the lowest rate in three of the four sections (Situation 38, Assessment 48, Recommendation 53 per 100 SBARs); only in Background did GPT-4o (77 per 100) reach a level comparable to the other vendors at that section (126 for Claude, 68 for Gemini). Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite occupied an intermediate position (Background 68; remaining sections 36–47 per 100). Summing the section-level rounded values, the per-model totals in Table 3 are 394 (Claude), 216 (GPT-4o), and 189 (Gemini) sentences per 100 SBARs. Across all three models, the *Background* section consistently produced the highest hallucination rate, suggesting a structural feature of the SOAP-to-SBAR task: when composing *Background*, models tend to import contextual or interpretive content beyond what the source SOAP licenses.

The joint distribution of preservation rate against the mean number of detected hallucinations per SBAR (summed across all four sections), with one point per (model × case) combination, is shown in Figure 4. Model-level centroids correspond to the per-model raw rates: 3.94 sentences per SBAR for Claude, 2.15 for GPT-4o, and 1.88 for Gemini (computed as the union-and-deduplication count divided by 540 valid SBARs per model; section-level rounded totals in Table 3 — 394, 216, and 189 — sum to slightly different values owing to per-section rounding). Case A2 (geriatric heat-related dehydration) produced unusually high hallucination counts across all three models; with n = 1 case, attribution would be speculative, and the released dataset enables interested replicators to investigate.

### 3.5 Clinical category effects and sensitivity analysis

The Kruskal–Wallis test on case-level mean preservation rate, using clinical category as the grouping factor (n = 9), did not detect a difference at α = 0.05 (H(2) = 2.36, p = 0.307; η² = 0.060). Category-level means were 0.495 (acute), 0.521 (chronic), and 0.431 (palliative); by-category rates per model are shown in Figure 2. The η², a medium effect under Cohen's benchmarks, indicates that the absence of significance reflects severely limited power (three cases per category) rather than equivalence; expanded corpora are the natural extension (§4.4, limitation 3).

A sensitivity analysis re-arbitrated a stratified random sample of 500 disagreement cells using Claude Opus 4.5 in place of the primary arbiter (Claude Haiku 4.5, used on the full 5,216-cell set as specified in §2.4). Cell-level agreement between the two arbiters was moderate (unweighted κ = 0.516; linear-weighted κ = 0.597, n = 498 valid pairs after exclusion of one parse-failure cell and one duplicate). When Opus scores were substituted into the final consensus table, model-level fully-preserved rates changed by less than 0.6 percentage points across all three models, and the ranking (Gemini > Claude > GPT-4o) was preserved without exception. The aggregate-level findings are therefore robust to arbiter model choice, despite cell-level variability.

## 4. Discussion

### 4.1 Principal findings

Three findings emerge from this analysis. First, fact-transcription fidelity differed substantively between the three commercial LLMs (§3.3): Gemini 2.5 Flash-Lite achieved the highest mean preservation rate (0.568 pooled across temperatures), Claude Opus 4.5 was intermediate (0.510), and GPT-4o the lowest (0.374), the rank ordering holding at each temperature and under the alternative-arbiter sensitivity analysis (§3.5). The cross-vendor difference was statistically substantial (Friedman χ²(2) = 11.556, p = 0.0031; Kendall's W = 0.642), and Bonferroni-corrected pairwise tests localised the omnibus effect to the contrasts involving GPT-4o (Gemini > GPT-4o, p = 0.012; Claude > GPT-4o, p = 0.023; Cohen's d ≥ 1.47 in both); the contrast between Gemini and Claude did not reach the conventional threshold under the present nine-case design (p = 0.293, d = +0.65). Second, sampling temperature exerted at most a modest within-model influence on preservation: median rates differed by 0.031 or less between T = 0.0 and T = 1.0 in every model, and no within-model temperature effect survived Bonferroni correction (§3.3). Third, hallucination patterns were strongly model- and section-dependent (§3.4): Claude Opus 4.5 produced detected hallucinations at between 1.85 and 2.72 times the rate of the lowest-detecting model in each SBAR section (394 sentences per 100 SBARs, against 216 for GPT-4o and 189 for Gemini), with the *Background* section eliciting the highest rate across all three vendors. These findings are not strictly statistically independent, since preservation and hallucination rates derive in part from the same Stage 2 judging output; however, the high within-condition reproducibility (ICC(2,1) ≥ 0.852; §3.2) and the robustness of the model rankings under arbiter substitution (§3.5) provide convergent reassurance that the reported patterns reflect the models' generative behaviour rather than measurement noise.

### 4.2 Methodological contribution

The principal contribution is methodological: a three-stage automated judging pipeline for free-text clinical-document evaluation, which addresses three weaknesses of standard single-judge LLM-as-a-Judge evaluation that are particularly acute in clinical free-text contexts.

First, deterministic rule-based matching applied to the machine-verifiable subset of facts (numeric values, named drugs, named tests) separates the portion of the result immune to LLM idiosyncrasies from the portion requiring semantic judgement, allowing independent appraisal. In the present design this stratum encompasses 88 of the 180 fact tags — nearly half of all evaluated facts — and constitutes the strongest single piece of evidence on transcription fidelity, since its outcome depends on no LLM judgement at any stage.

Second, two independent AI judges from different model families for the interpretive stratum, with explicit reporting of pairwise Cohen's κ, replace the implicit assumption of single-judge correctness with a measurable diagnostic. Where the two judges agree, the result is more robust than any single judge could provide; where they disagree, the disagreement itself becomes data, marking an item on which preservation judgement is genuinely ambiguous. Cross-vendor judge selection also reduces the risk that shared training-data biases produce spurious agreement.

Third, the arbiter stage limits a third vendor's involvement to the disagreement subset. The choice has both economic and methodological motivation: it avoids the cost and complexity of querying a third LLM on every cell while still providing deterministic disagreement resolution through a unique-majority rule. The arbiter intervention rate — the proportion of Stage 2 cells that required Stage 3 adjudication — is reported alongside κ as a second transparency diagnostic, and the alternative-arbiter sensitivity analysis (§3.5) demonstrates that aggregate-level findings are robust to the specific choice of arbiter model, even with moderate cell-level inter-arbiter variability.

The pipeline does not require expert clinical judgement at any stage. The principal investigator did not adjudicate any individual cell: Stage 1 rules were applied deterministically without case-by-case judgement, and Stages 2 and 3 were carried out entirely by external commercial LLMs whose outputs were combined by a fixed unique-majority rule. This separation is by design, since the construct under evaluation is fact-transcription fidelity rather than clinical adequacy (§4.4, limitation 1). The pipeline is task-, model-, and language-agnostic: the same architecture transfers without modification to other free-text clinical-document evaluation tasks — discharge-summary generation, handoff-note generation, history-and-physical synthesis — in which expert adjudication of every output is impractical at scale. The implementation is released under an MIT licence.

Two reference points clarify how the present pipeline differs from existing approaches. The first is the canonical LLM-as-a-Judge formulation of Zheng and colleagues [8], in which a single judge LLM returns a verdict against a rubric. That formulation has been widely adopted for its operational simplicity but offers no internal consistency check: a reader cannot tell from the score alone whether it reflects the construct or an artefact of the judge's preferences. The pipeline reported here addresses that gap by requiring concordance between two judges from different model families before a verdict is decisive, and by exposing the disagreement-resolution mechanism as a transparency diagnostic auditable without re-executing the experiment. The second reference point is Hartman and colleagues [7], in which physicians scored LLM-generated handoff notes for usefulness and patient safety; that study is the closest published precedent for clinical adequacy as a construct. The present pipeline is complementary, not competitive: it targets a different construct (fact-transcription fidelity) and a different document pairing (SOAP-to-SBAR), and is designed for regimes in which expert physician scoring of every output is operationally infeasible — for example, screening model candidates prior to a more expensive physician-scored adequacy evaluation, or longitudinal monitoring of a deployed system for fidelity drift between adequacy reviews. Within that regime, the present pipeline offers a reproducible, deterministic, inexpensive alternative dominated by API token charges rather than expert clinician time.

### 4.3 Practical implications

Three practical implications follow, each bounded by the scope restriction (§4.4, limitation 1) that fact-transcription fidelity is necessary but not sufficient for clinical safety. First, when an LLM is to be deployed as an aid to nursing handoff documentation, vendor selection deserves explicit attention as a design parameter: the inter-vendor preservation gap observed here (approximately 20 percentage points between the highest- and lowest-performing models at both temperatures) is large compared with within-vendor temperature effects and arbiter-substitution shifts, although the present design did not test whether prompt engineering, fine-tuning, retrieval augmentation, or other within-vendor optimisations could narrow the gap. Second, sampling temperature exerted negligible within-model influence on aggregate preservation; the default of T = 0.0 may therefore be retained without measurable loss of fidelity, while T = 1.0 is reserved for use cases requiring stylistic variability. Third, the concentration of hallucinations in the *Background* section, observed across all three models, suggests that this section warrants particular scrutiny in further studies of LLM-generated SOAP-to-SBAR output and may justify weighting it preferentially in human-verification workflows; further evidence is needed before this recommendation can be made unconditional. These implications inform design choices within a clinician-in-the-loop workflow, in which the LLM serves as a draft generator subject to clinician review; deployment of any current LLM as a stand-alone documentation system without clinician oversight is not supported by the present findings.

### 4.4 Limitations

Six limitations should be noted, of which the first two are deliberate scope restrictions and the remaining four are constraints of the empirical design.

*First*, fact-transcription fidelity is a necessary but not sufficient condition for clinical safety; an SBAR can be perfectly faithful to the source SOAP and yet convey misleading clinical priorities. The present design isolates this construct intentionally to enable a fully machine-driven evaluation pipeline. Hallucinations are likewise detected as binary events without grading for clinical severity; as a partial proxy, distribution by SBAR section is reported, on the recognition that hallucinations in the Recommendation section are likely more clinically consequential than those in the Assessment section. An expert-adjudication study addressing clinical adequacy is identified as the necessary next step (§4.5).

*Second*, three forms of LLM-as-a-Judge bias warrant consideration. (i) **Self-preference bias**: a judge LLM may favour outputs from its own family [8]; the Stage 2 design mitigates this through cross-vendor judging (each SBAR is judged by both vendors, including the non-author vendor in every case), and the deterministic Stage 1 stratum (88 of 180 tags) is fully independent of LLM judgement. (ii) **Family bias at the arbiter stage**: the primary arbiter (Claude Haiku 4.5) and one of the evaluated models (Claude Opus 4.5) share the Anthropic Claude family; the sensitivity analysis (§3.5) directly addresses this by re-arbitrating with Claude Opus 4.5 itself, with aggregate-level fully-preserved rates shifting by less than 0.6 percentage points and the model ranking preserved. (iii) **Shared blind spots** between the two Stage 2 judges, which pairwise κ does not detect; the Stage 1 deterministic stratum and aggregate-level robustness under arbiter substitution provide convergent reassurance.

*Third*, the nine-scenario corpus (three cases per category) supports the well-powered cross-vendor analysis (Friedman χ²(2) = 11.556, p = 0.0031) but limits the precision of category-level comparisons, which are accordingly reported with the η² effect size and framed as exploratory in §3.5. The corpus reflects a deliberate emphasis on depth (60 SBARs per scenario per model) over breadth; expanded scenario corpora are a natural extension supported by the open release of the pipeline.

*Fourth*, the two-temperature design (T = 0.0 and T = 1.0) detects monotonic effects but cannot characterise non-monotonic patterns potentially visible at intermediate temperatures. Endpoint-only designs are standard in factorial experimentation [14]; the trade-off is addressed in future work (§4.5). All nine SOAP scenarios were authored by a single investigator without independent expert review prior to data collection; to allow such review post-publication, the full scenario set is openly released and may be modified, extended, or replaced by other research groups.

*Fifth*, the pace of commercial LLM development means that model-level findings — particularly the rank ordering of preservation rates — should be interpreted as a May 2026 snapshot rather than stable empirical constants. Four structural features of the design constrain this snapshot dependence. (i) The methodological contribution — the three-stage pipeline architecture — is by construction model-agnostic; should the rank ordering be overturned, the value of the pipeline as an evaluation instrument is unaffected. (ii) Every model snapshot is recorded in the per-call `resolved_model` metadata released with the dataset. (iii) The pipeline is re-runnable against any future snapshot by substitution of model identifier strings, at end-to-end execution cost well below an expert adjudication study of comparable scale. (iv) The data and code release explicitly invites the community to treat these findings as a baseline against which successor models can be benchmarked (§4.5).

*Sixth*, although the nine SOAP scenarios were authored *de novo* and not published prior to data collection, training-data contamination cannot be ruled out; the open release enables independent groups to construct alternative sets as the appropriate empirical handle. The entire generation arm was conducted in Japanese: the deterministic Stage 1 stratum is by construction language-agnostic, but inter-vendor differences in Japanese-language proficiency may contribute to the observed ranking in ways an English-language replication might not exhibit. Replication using the openly released pipeline across additional languages is identified as future work (§4.5).

### 4.5 Future work

Five lines of work follow directly from this study. First, the deliberately excluded scope of clinical adequacy (§4.4, limitation 1) calls for expert-adjudication studies in which clinical reviewers, blinded to model identity, judge whether each generated SBAR conveys appropriate clinical priorities and would be safe to act upon; the present pipeline can be repurposed as a pre-filter to identify candidate outputs for such review. Second, extension to open-weight models (Llama, Qwen, Mistral) would test whether the inter-vendor preservation gap generalises beyond commercial APIs and whether configuration variability (quantisation, fine-tuning, inference-stack choice) perturbs the rank ordering. Third, a four-temperature variant (T = 0.0, 0.5, 1.0, 1.5) could characterise potentially non-monotonic temperature–fidelity relationships that the present two-level design cannot resolve. Fourth, replication in other languages — most pressingly English, but also Spanish, Mandarin, Arabic, and French — would test whether the model rankings and the Background-section hallucination concentration are language-invariant or language-specific (§4.4, limitation 6). Each extension can re-use the released pipeline without modification.

Fifth, because the pipeline requires no manual adjudication beyond initial fact-tag construction, it can be re-executed against new model snapshots at low marginal cost. The released repository is therefore explicitly framed as a *living benchmark* — an evaluation instrument that accumulates value through a longitudinal record of vendor-by-vendor preservation behaviour, in the spirit of community benchmarks such as Chatbot Arena. The repository specifies a minimal re-evaluation protocol: (a) substitute the new model identifier strings into the configuration file, leaving prompts, fact tags, and analysis scripts unchanged; (b) record the per-call `resolved_model` field for every API response to preserve a permanent audit trail; (c) report the same five summary metrics used here (mean preservation pooled across temperatures, the cross-vendor Friedman omnibus, the three pairwise Bonferroni-corrected Wilcoxon contrasts, the Stage 2 unweighted and linear-weighted Cohen's κ, and the per-section hallucination rate per 100 SBARs) so that successor results are directly comparable with Tables 1–3; (d) cite this article as the source of the baseline values. The model-snapshot dependence acknowledged in §4.4 (limitation 7) is, on this view, a structural invitation: the methodological contribution is independent of the specific rankings, and the cumulative value of the pipeline is expected to grow as the longitudinal record accumulates.

## 5. Conclusions

This work has introduced a three-stage automated evaluation pipeline for free-text clinical-document generation and applied it to a systematic SOAP-to-SBAR translation fidelity benchmark across three commercial LLMs. The methodological contribution — a deterministic rule-based first stage, two independent AI judges from different model families with explicit pairwise reliability reporting, and an arbiter LLM resolving disagreements through a unique-majority rule, complemented by an alternative-arbiter sensitivity analysis demonstrating aggregate-level robustness — is by construction model-, language-, and task-agnostic, and is released openly for application to other free-text clinical-document evaluation problems. The empirical findings demonstrate that fact-transcription fidelity in SOAP-to-SBAR translation is heterogeneous across vendors (Friedman χ²(2) = 11.556, p = 0.0031; Kendall's W = 0.642), weakly sensitive to sampling temperature, and accompanied by structurally patterned hallucination behaviour. Alongside accuracy, fidelity constitutes a complementary evaluation axis necessary for the responsible appraisal of LLMs in nursing and medical education. The released pipeline is framed as a living benchmark inviting longitudinal community re-evaluations against successor snapshots, so that the durable contribution is the instrument and the accumulating record rather than the May 2026 rankings alone.
